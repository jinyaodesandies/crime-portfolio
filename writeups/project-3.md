# U.S. Crime Trends

## Write-Up

The dashboard Trends in US Violent and Property Crime, 1960-2022 describes the temporal
changes in crime trends within the USA during the period. Using the FBI Uniform Crime
Reporting Database’s collection of various crimes rates from across local precincts, which were
rated to a per 100,000 basis, three visualizations were created to provide a regional and type
breakdown of crime trends. First, A line chart was created to show changes in murder rate by
region. Then, an interactive scatter plot was made to compare violent crime with property crime
over time. Lastly, a focus was placed on various types of property crimes in Florida, with a
stacked bar chart showing comparable statistics to the overall national trend.

The line chart utilizes the measures murder rate and region. The diagram compares these two
measures over the 1960–2022-time span. The murder rate, rated to a per 100,000 basis, reflects
the differing rate of crime between different regions in the US. The South tops the national
comparison in murder rate, and the Northeast carries the lowest murder rate over most of the
period. For the two other regions, while initially murder was higher in the US West, eventually it
was superseded by the Midwest. A special point of note is the Supreme Court moratorium on
capital punishment between 1972-1976, as shown in the reference band (Bomboy, 2023).

Although the chart suggests that crime peaked then fell during the period, research shows that the
effect was likely minimal. On one hand, researchers argue that increased executions may have
deterred crime because of the deterrence effect, where fear for the punishment leading to death
stops individuals from committing crime (Shephard,2005). On the other hand, with data from the
moratorium Leamer found in 1983 that executions drove an increase in violent crime due to the
brutalization effect, where the state legitimizes violent behavior by itself executing individuals,
driving an uptick in crime. That explains both why crime instead peaked during the moratorium
and decreased both during and far after the national ban on capital punishment.

The interactive scatter plot describes the change in both violent and property crime in all US
states, over time. Each state, represented by a diamond, was sized based on its population and
colored based on the region. The chart is made interactive through a “By Year” time tool which
allowed both over-time trend plays and selection of specific years. The chart shows that crime
uniformly increased until 1980 in most states and then decreased up until the most recent year of
data collection. Research attributes the sudden decrease in crime to legalized abortion and a
decrease in teen pregnancies. Less individuals born under crime-driving impoverishment and
neglect from circumstances leading to abortions or teen pregnancies. This likely decreased
occurrences of crime (Donohue & Levitt, 2001). However, a Pew report that summarizes trends
in crime describe individuals across the nation still believed that crime was rising across the
nation, except in their area (Gramlich, 2024). The finding matches the scatter plot’s description
of a general decrease in crime in all states up until 2022, corresponding perceptions of local
crime decreasing but contradicting the perception of rising crime nationally.

Lastly, a stacked bar chart focuses on Florida to dissect the different types of property crimes
commonly committed. The stacked bar chart reflects national trends in property crime, with two
peaks around 1980 and 1990. Compared nationally Florida generally lies in the top quartile of
states for property crime. Broken down by type, Larceny, or theft of personal property, occurred
more than all other crimes combined. It was followed by burglary, or unlawful entry into
properties for the purpose of crime, and then vehicle theft. It is of note that data is likely distorted
by differing reporting rates. For example, 88% of car thefts were often reported while only 44%
of burglaries were. The discrepancy is in part because police place low level of significance in
solving lower-value burglaries, leading to less individuals reporting (Gramlich, 2024). Due to the
reliance of the data source on the Uniform Crime Reporting System, which relies on police data-
it may be distorted to show comparatively more violent crime out of total occurrences (Federal
Bureau of Investigation, 2023).

In conclusion, national crime increased in all forms during the 1980s and 1990s and then
decreased in part due to less unwanted pregnancies. The visualization also reflects how
punishment likely had discharging effects on murder- which is demonstrated by how murder
rates both peaked and began to decline during the moratorium. There are many different types of
crimes that are committed, It Is important to note that because of different clearing rates and
reporting rates, some crimes may appear to be less prevalent than they appear to be.
