# Common Crimes in Florida’s University Campuses

## Write-Up

The State of Crime in Florida’s Top Five Colleges project utilizes the Campus Safety
and Security dataset provided by the Department of Education (DoE) to visualize and
analyze common crimes in five college campuses in Florida. These colleges were chosen
because they had the highest enrollment in the state(DoE, 2025).
Data was downloaded from source as a CSV and connected to Tableau desktop.
Each row of the CSV was a statistical observation in the form of crime tallies at a specific
institution in a specific year. Rape, fondling, incest, statutory rape, aggravated assault, and
burglary were the statistical parameters chosen for this project. They were used to create
three figures: A line chart describing the trend of sexual oMenses over time, a treemap
highlighting the volume of aggravated assault at each school, and a lollipop chart
illustrating the frequency of burglary at each institution.

The line chart describes Sexual Misconduct at Florida's Top 5 Universities from 2019
to 2023. For visual simplicity, all forms of sexual misconduct (rape, fondling, incest and
statutory rape) were grouped together as sexual assault using the SUM tool. These
measures differ in the specific form in which assault occurred: whereas rape is the
penetration of the vagina or anus, fondling refers to all touching of private body parts of
another unwilling person for the purpose of sexual gratification. Statutory rape is a specific
designation for non-forcible sexual intercourse with a person who is under the statutory
age of consent. Incest is the sexual intercourse between two individuals where marriage is
illegal by law (DoE, 2025). Purdum with the New York Times reported that sexual assault
became increasingly prevalent on college campuses, a situation worsened by colleges
refusing to escalate reported cases (Purdum, 1988). Grogger’s (1991) rational crime model
notes that certainty of crime is a far better deterrent compared to the severity of
punishment. The underreporting phenomenon drives low certainty of punishment, which
leaves less expected cost of crime, possibly leading to more sexual assault. More recently,
Fenda et al (2016) reviews that sexual assault not only remains to be prevalent on college
campuses but vary widely across colleges. Some schools pay less attention to non-white
non-heterosexual female victims. Thus, differing identity compositions may explain the
discrepancy in sex oMense reporting rather than a difference in frequency of occurrence.
Nonetheless, sex crime in Florida is less severe than the national trend, with most schools
between 20-40 below the national average of 74 per 100,000 (NCES, 2023).

The treemap describes the scale of aggravated assault at each campus. Aggravated
assault is an attack on another person. Aggravated assault is a felony form of assault
involving the possession of a weapon (DoE, 2025). The size of each box depends on a
measure originating from the aggravated assault column of the CSV file, rated to a per
100,000 basis and averaged over the span of 5 years. It is noteworthy that the University of
Florida (Florida, UF) had more than five times more aggravated assault than the institution
with the second most cases of aggravated assault. This is in part a product of its central
location in Gainesville, which was notably known for a homelessness and violent crime
crisis in the observed time interval (see Figure 1)(Lunn, 2021). Aggravated assault is
generally the least common of the three crimes studied in terms of national averages, with
only about 15 per 100,000 students (NCES, 2016). This highlights FU’s violence problem:
aggravated assault is doubly as common in Florida. Indeed, the availability of comparable
data demonstrated to Gainesville authorities a need to address the issue. Multiple
measures were passed to decrease the rate of violent crime, coming to a noticeable
decrease in violent crime as in 2025 (Tuberville 2026).

Lastly, The lollipop chart describes the average level of burglary on college
campuses. Burglary is the unlawful entry of a structure to with criminal intent (DoE).
Campuses are dense, have predictable resident schedules, as well as high yearly
turnover(Soldato, 2025). Each of these create a lower probability for criminals to be
convicted, decreasing certainty of crime. Location also matters: universities such as FIU
and UCF on the edge of population centers exhibited the lowest level of burglaries, colleges
like Florida and FSU which are the center local population density experienced more than
double the amount of burglary compared to the next highest institution (see Figure 1).
Despite the prevalence of assault in UF and FSU, Florida schools still have significantly
lower burglary rates than the national average of 52.3 per 100,000. The difference suggests
that the State of Florida has relatively less property crime compared to the US.
The data demonstrates that the rate of crime is generally higher in schools located in
population centers, especially specialized college towns. Rate of sexual misconduct
reporting also depends on institutional culture beyond the actual rate of occurrence,
highlighting a need for increased education on sexual violence. 
Ultimately, Florida’s universities are generally safer in most aspects to national standards, 
suggesting a general level of success in campus security measures despite drastic internal differences.
