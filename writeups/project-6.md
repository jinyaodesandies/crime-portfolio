# Independent Project: Crimes Against Humanity

## Write-Up

The Tableau dashboard Crimes Against Humanity allows users to interact with data on crimes
against humanity occurring between 2018 and 2022 provided by the Perceived Mass Atrocities
Dataset (Meisel et al. 2024). A treemap outlines the regions and countries with the most common
occurrences of these crimes. For more detail into specific countries, a map selector then filters
for the types of crime committed and victims of the crime, presented in the form of a stacked
area chart and a pie chart.

For the treemap, the size of rectangles representing the number of atrocities perceived to have
been committed per million residents. Atrocities include various crimes against humanity as
defined by the UN: forced conscription, mass containment, forced displacement, erasure, less-
than-lethal attacks, mass murder, and reduced citizenship (Yale Genocide Studies Program,
2024). These are labeled containment, displacement, erasure, less-lethal attacks, murder, and
reduced citizenship in the dashboard for conciseness. These are all crimes against humanity
because they involve purposeful destruction of a people group through inflictions on the
conditions of their life. The data was summed to create the main measure for this dashboard,
atrocity magnitude. The wording “perceived” is used because often atrocities are disputed in an
authoritative manner (Meisel et al., 2024). This highlights a key challenge in data collection for
crimes against humanity: state actors vehemently deny and often engage in disinformation
campaigns to, avoiding responsibility for their actions (Sudworth, 2021). This links to the
rational model of crime discussed in class. If there is no risk to be prosecuted, there will simply
be no deterrence to crime, and rational state criminals are therefore incentivized to continue
committing them (Becker, 1974).

Below the treemap is a stacked area chart, which shows changes in the occurrence of crime over
time. Often, magnitude of these crimes can spike because of events such as conflict or legislative
change. A common type of atrocity is displacement, where residents are forced to leave their
home due to some sort of status. In this case, displacement is sponsored by the state to make
room for settlers, such as the Israeli settlement of the West Bank (Al Jazeera, 2026). An example
of, for purposes of war, such as Russian forced evacuations of Ukrainian civilians (OECD,
2026). Furthermore, displacement triggers long term negative social, economic, and health
consequences by removing people groups from their established property, shredding community
ties and shared history. Like terrorism discussed in class, often individual cases of displacement
have larger impacts on civilian populations because it adds a mental toll to individuals not yet
impacted, as well as driving them to self-displace. This forms an extra benefit to the criminals,
further incentivizing them to commit more of these atrocities. This highlights the importance of
International bodies like the International Court of Transitional Justice. The court which
supposedly has international jurisdiction is making efforts to clarify these acts as humanitarian
crimes, allowing for future prosecution and deterrence (Andreu-Guzmán, 2013).

Next to the stacked area chart lies the pie chart, which highlights the distribution of atrocity
types, or crimes against humanity, in selected countries. Furthermore, tooltips reveal the
perpetrator and victim of crimes. Common perpetrators include national police, terrorist groups,
and even ethnic mobs. Victims are often targeted because of their political status or religious
beliefs. As shown in the pie chart, countries with a lot of atrocities have reduced citizenship or
displacement as the most common crimes committed. Reduced citizenship crimes involve
stripping rights from longtime residents, creating a second-class citizenship with less rights and
benefits due to some sort of ancestry. These are considered crimes against humanity because they
render residents’ refugees in their own country, with no “home” to ever enjoy full rights as
human beings (Worster, 2019). However, like displacement, reduced citizenship is often ignored
by international criminal justice bodies, in large part because the actor is most commonly state
level, therefore generally having sovereignty over who to consider a citizen (Baughman, 2026).

In conclusion, the Crimes Against Humanity dashboard outlines the countries with most
egregious occurrences of atrocities against victim groups, as well as the types of crime and
chronological progression of occurrences. These visualizations shine light on egregious acts of
physical or mental violence that either legitimate or proclaimed state actors place upon their own
subjects. Optimally, those driving these actions deserve prosecution and final reckoning with real
consequences.
