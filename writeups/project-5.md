# Global Terrorism

## Write-Up

Terrorism is a global phenomenon that is vaguely defines as sub-national acts of violence. It is
generally meant to incite fear among a society through large scale acts of violence, often to
innocent individuals (FBI 2025). The Tableau dashboard Terrorism Around the World visualizes
various aspects of global terrorism. Namely, three diagrams utilizing the Global Terrorism
Database collected by Miller et al. in 2020. The first diagram is a packed bubble chart that
summarizes the number of terrorist attacks in each unique province or state over recorded time
span from 1970 to 2020. The second, a proportional area chart, shows how the amount each
region composed of total terrorist attacks over time changes. The third, a stacked bar chart,
breaks down the distribution of attack types in each region. Together, they reveal the underlying
complexities of terrorism- how, where and when they occur.
The packed bubble chart is a representation of the relationship between provinces and states and
occurrences of terrorist attacks. Namely, that some provinces or states have an exceeding number
of attacks, which is represented by the size of the circles. Some hotbeds of attacks such as
Baghdad, Sindh and Northern Ireland, have been related by significant policymakers and
economists as being caused by poverty and lack of job opportunities (Bush 2002) (Krueger
2001). This is linked to the opportunity cost theory of crime: more income made by individuals
is more to lose from death or imprisonment linked to terrorism. However, quantitative analysis
suggests otherwise. Terrorism was conducted by a span of individuals with varying levels of
education and income and did not necessarily increase during times of economic struggle (Green,
Glaser, and Rich 1998).
The proportional area chart describes the proportion of terrorist attacks that occurred in each
region. It reveals the regional density of terrorism, or how terrorism attacks occurred more in
specific regions in response to specific events. Note that spikes in instances of terrorism were
followed by falling figures. Spikes of note include western Europe, driven in large part by the
Irish independence movement, and the Middle East, driven by a rise in Islamic Fundamentalism.
Whereas the former was resolved via diplomatic solutions, which the chart shows as drastically
effective, the latter was confronted through the War on Terror, which may have been effective: a
given country was upwards of 60% more likely to experience a terrorism incident prior to or
during the year 2001 than after (Smith and Zeigler 2017).
The stacked bar chart breaks down the type of terrorist attacks common to different regions. For
all regions, bombing is most common, generally followed by armed assault or infrastructure
attacks. These are chosen for their maximal effect of terror, driving all productive members of
society to develop fear for becoming the next target, hampering productivity to drive political
change. Some acts of terror may have been successful. Although in the short term, bombings
may not have a significant effect on human productivity, in the long term it led to an overreaction
towards immigration, arousal-anxiety from stimuli of the event for a large population, and
general fear in the daily lives of residents (Silver 2002). Therefore, it truly depends on the actual
goals of the terrorist to determine if the operation has any actual effectiveness.
Specific to the state of Florida, the final visualization is a density map showing the exact
locations in which terrorism occurs. It is of note that most terrorist attacks occur near large
metropolitan areas, as signified by larger circles in the area. Large areas are often denser, offering
opportunities to conduct significant amounts of damage. Furthermore, they are often more
politically progressive, which leads to controversy that drives individuals to violence. For
example, the reasonably dense city of Oakland park experienced a firebombing in 2000. The
firebombing was towards an abortion clinic but lead to no deaths and therefore no long-term
effects on practices of abortion in the state (Sun Sentinel 2000) (Florida Agency for Health Care
Administration 2025). In the context of Apuzzo’s 2016 analysis, community-based intervention
and conversation may have prevented this arson, which was conducted in a way where harm to
individuals was not possible and rather only acted as an expression of anger. This highlights the
importance of community investment and dialogue to prevent terrorism.
In conclusion, occupation and income does not affect one’s tendency to terrorism, while
diplomacy and warfare may have each been effective solutions. Florida’s existence of terrorism
was most common in urban areas and are ideologically driven. Possibly through community
intervention, these could also be mitigated and prevented.
